Something of Use

Winter — Exegesis

2026

Around Southeast DC in the 90s, a woman in her early twenties emerged as one of the area’s most celebrated hairdressers. Impressive, too, is that Tyra Hunter, the woman in question, came out as trans in 1985, aged 15. A nontrivial feat. Why do I know all this? It’s private.

Private. In part. But, also, the nearest-to-honest answer is: “I don’t know.” Whatever went on to cause Hunter’s explosion into the fame she, acquaintances say, swore was her destiny is not something I understand. The course of events is well documented, well evidenced, and easily recounted. But, as it goes with the youngest New Franciscans – to whom Josef cannot relay the cruelness of a blood rain, cannot make it make sense – the story ‘doesn’t hold in the mind.’ The New Franciscan elder might ask the young to show mercy, to elect their survival above their pride and remember the cost of a blood rain. But may it not be the blood rain’s illegibility that galvanises, that inspires young Pin to mock the suicide of the Atmospherean academic or to execute Marigail? Regarding Tyra Hunter, Snorton & Haritaworn write of…

…lives structured alternately by illegibility and spectacle. Those lives also carry a productive force – particularly in death – that sheds light on the borders where biopower and necropower brush against each another in everyday life. (2013)

This simultaneity of illegibility and spectacle allows for the production of horror without the risk of intergenerational transmission. Great enough traumas, even/especially should they fuel the distribution of their own spectacle, nonetheless do not transmit as and are not distributed as trauma. Such an experience’s productive function, so, does not match with its function for the one who experiences it. Winter:

Among the blood that fell on Josef, got in his mouth, was any his son's? … The genius.

And:

Young people were told, shown records, but there is no describing, no explaining, a Blood Rain: always the genius of it.

And Pin:

Pin knew Josef’s story, knew of the blood rains. To a lover one night, Pin mocked the old man’s moral. “If this woman were an ally,” Pin said, “there’d be no replacement. She’d bring the city down. At least take some out. Do something of use.”

Testimonies of death are by necessity extractive. Of course, the only value they hold is for the living. Some deaths, also, are more valuable than others: “trans deaths—and most frequently the deaths of trans women or trans-feminine people of color—act as a resource for the development and dissemination of many different agendas” (Snorton & Haritaworn). The especial economic attractiveness of the deaths of trans women/transfeminine people of colour, in this case, owes to the comparative costs of their experiences’ extraction while alive versus while dead. To a cis lens, for example, trans lives are illegible and difficult to find any use for. In death, though, all at once, that same illegibility becomes usable; in dying, a trans woman is elevated from dead capital to formal capital. The more spectacular trans deaths bring tremendous market activity, and as such are welcomed with rugs, feasted at galas. S&H:

Immobilized in life, and barred from spaces designated as white (the good life, the Global North, the gentrifying inner city, the university, the trans community), it is in their death that poor and sex working trans people of color are invited back in; it is in death that they suddenly come to matter.

Winter reports on the mechanics of necropolitics without reproducing or feeding its economic machine, a difficult balance nonetheless struck by means of nonrepresentative allegory. When Winter’s apocalypse fell, its survivors retreated not into underground but overground bunkers. To the sky. Meanwhile, the irradiated remnants of ground-level humanity gather in anarchist communes, producing their own systems of violence and mechanisms for death, albeit unspoken systems. As per:

“And the rules?”

“There aren’t rules.” …

“If not rules, it’s – what? – first impressions? My survival depends on a winning disposition, a firm handshake?”

“You can trade. Try to get along with people…”

And:

“With jails you go in, you come out. … Without them everything’s prison: you’re always in it.”

Marigail’s complaint proves prophetic, when she meets Pin, who asks Josef, “Do I have the right?” Indeed, Marigail’s survival depended on first impressions. See, also, the academic’s suicide as narrativised by Josef. The academic’s life has use and meaning for Josef only in her taking it. Almost every character is eyeing wins they might, should they think of it, gain by the death of any other. Thereby, Winter presents a resource-report on death, its uses for those who survive, and the social technologies by which it is assigned and negotiated for or against. One atrocity represented in Apocalypse Now is met with the response, “the genius of that, the genius,” and, in the same motion, with refrains to the effect: “the horror, the horror.” The horror and the genius are one. The task is to arrive at this juncture of negated expression and, by allegory but not representation, give expression to it. Nonrepresentative allegory serves in this instance, thus, as my own gignobatic gesture, a departure from what is otherwise forsaken or already-dead, through strict sci-fi genre-unrealism/fantasy, toward the speaking into recognition and, by extension, reality of a maligned fragment of ontology. A right hand turn off the left hand path: a gignobasis.

Horse Breeders — Exegesis

2026

Pick which opening paragraph you prefer.

A tree falls in earshot. A tree falls in sight. For myself, these trees are different; are not the same. But, if you're blind, the tree-in-earshot has nothing to compare to. No second tree to be 'not the same as.' So, the tree celebrates a rare independence, gets to exist, non-relationally, in and of itself. So, there exist scenarios such that a lack of sense/comprehension produces witnesses that are the right kind for the moment. Ask, as such, what Muchi sees while most do not.

What kind of clownworld brainrot contagion has led you, home viewer, to this reality in which you've now read the words, "Genghis Khan's womanhood"? We're all going to the world's fair in very deed. In any case, re: Genghis Khan's womanhood (as expressed in Muchi): its key interest is its 'clownworld' incomprehensibility. By embodying (in her 'authentic' selfhood) the great khan's womanhood, Muchi becomes a carrier for the clownworld-contagion. As such, she is cut off from herself. She cannot relate to her own embodiment.

Sometimes, when nothing sticks together, the cosmos looks formless, sometimes, you grab one thing you know the name of and find another thing with the same name. You hold them together in either hand, count them: One in my left. One in my right. … Repeat until you're sure it works the way it used to: One and One still make Two.

But other times, when nothing sticks, the cosmos looks formless, you sink. That's all you do. It goes like that because when you open your eyes, there's nothing you know the name of. What can you grab two of? Nothing touches anything else.

And (ibid.):

In his frustration with the cosmos, the absurdity of his own gangling, animal body, Lovecraft has sought to express himself through descriptive metaphor. His Yith [conical, half-crab, half-coral people] are a visual permutation of Cassandra's ὀτοτοτοῖ πόποι δᾶ: a chaos of limbs and animal parts thrown together out of infuriated indifference.

How does one perpetuate one’s life in the multiversal timeline where one is assigned Genghis Khan-at-birth and becomes a woman? These categories, by rights, do not touch. Only in a kind of clownworld-reality could they touch. Yet, here, in the world Muchi must inhabit instant-by-instant, they touch. So, she and we are in Clownworld. Where’s the closest laundromat? What about the medical centre?

Muchi seeks some initial means of re-orienting herself in relation to this extra-realist world and, thereby, re-grounding her selfhood within it. She seeks some new point of contact, but has only other people’s data – cis people’s – to pave the way. It is inarticulable (therefore unthinkable) that being the great khan, being a clone – all of that busy and peculiar detail – might have nothing to do with it. To be trans without a sense of herself as trans, as sharing some social belonging has – maybe – disabled her comprehension of these multiple transitions (i.e. from the Temuchin body to the Muchi body, a motion, a forgotten passage through a realm invisible to cis lenses). She suspects an error at the core of her being, and this arrested self-expression may be it. By an excess of pre-loaded information about one’s social being, one’s comprehension of the self is cluttered to obscurity, such that – in Muchi’s case – she cannot see the still truth of her self-/trans-embodiment, the one piece that would make her articulable to herself, even if no longer articulable to most others, i.e., most cis people. The clutter – the ‘busy and particular detail’ – of Muchi’s epistemic stance is only compounded by the attraction a phrase like ‘Genghis Khan’s womanhood’ must hold for Orientalist misreadings/misreaders.

Representations of dysphoria and third genders in non-Western contexts often exoticise, rendering as spectacle rather than as medical condition and epistemology. The fascination is with the unthreatening, looking-glassed Other, whose children your children will never meet and, less still, at whose home they will never ask to sleep over. But who is producing these representations? Certainly Western and Westernised lenses exoticise and have exoticised non-Western contexts, made scare-mirrors of them in order to reproduce their own identities, have (per Said) Orientalised them. Yet, spectacles hide within spectacles. Why not allow that representations of dysphoria in non-Western contexts are looking-glassed and othered by local systems, already and well in advance of the anthropologists’ arrival? The local integrations undertaken by ethnographers are, give or take, processes by which one becomes an upright citizen in good standing with the community and the subject of one’s study. Hands up who’s been raped by an upright citizen in good standing with their community. The problem – i.e. looking at such contexts from without is to look through a reduplicated fractal of skewed lenses – is obvious. You may, very easily, find someone from Mongolia who takes remarkable exception to my portrayal of Genghis Khan’s relationship with Jamuqa, not to mention the khan’s transformation into a woman. But, as anticolonial theorist Talia Bhatt, a trans and desi woman, has it, “Do you want to find a conservative marginalised woman? You will if you look for one” (Dec 2025). And (ibid.):

…the West is also a convenient rhetorical tool for conservatives looking to cast any and all oppressive practices as an indispensable cultural artifact that needs protection from ‘Western colonialism’, even—perhaps especially—when non-Western women and queer people are the ones speaking out against them.

And (Sep 2024):

…Western anthropological scholarship … has now been cited by an Indian court in an opinion that … purports that recognizing [Hijra] as women would ‘violate their constitutional rights’. It is seemingly only imperialism when populations who seek the technologies of transition and legible womanhood are granted access to them, while the opinions of Western academics shaping local politics is [sic.] merely sparkling scholarship.

Everybody who is or loves a trans person should read Talia Bhatt’s essay, The Third Sex. “How,” she asks in that essay, “can I express to you how hysteria-inducing it is to see the hijra described as revered, when I grew up immersed in the toxic miasma of that ‘reverence’?” [My emphasis.] The Third Sex is – in large part – a severe, tongue-lashing book review of Neither Man Nor Woman by anthropologist Serena Nanda. Bhatt accounts the frequency with which Nanda asked South Asia’s transfeminised women and hijras to nominate their gender, was answered, “I am a woman,” then recorded that answer verbatim before naming them “a religious community of men who dress and act like women” (1999, page 1). Nanda also relates a story in which an Indian trans woman insists on her womanhood, only to be rebuked by a local magistrate with: “Can you give birth to a baby? If that is not possible, then you don’t win.” Astonishingly, Nanda uses this tale (told by a hijra) as ‘evidence’ for the hijra’s wilful non-identification with womanhood. To her, the transfeminised are so invisible as to be invisible in their own storytelling, such that, even in their own speech, the voice of the oppressing hegemony is the only voice and must, therefore, be the intended expression of the tale. Scrambling for analogy, and scrambling to understand: imagine a plush puppeteered by a child. Its is the face of a bear, but the child ventriloquises it to say, “Behold. I am Lord Mousefrog, a mousefrog.” And the child says, “Can you hop on a lily-pad? No. Obvious. Dumb Mousefrog is a dogbear.” Here, a rational observer could, mistakenly but understandably, register Mousefrog as not a mousefrog but a dogbear who acts like a mousefrog, or even, allowing for the intrusion of no local lens at all, as still a plush bear, neither mousefrog nor dogbear. Such was the status Nanda granted that woman in her own story. Puppeteered plush of the Indian Supreme Court. Of the two, which of Genghis’s traits is most complicating to Muchi’s transness/trans womanhood? His maleness or his Mongolianness? Above all, the clutterings around Muchi’s needs as a trans woman trouble and obscure the mundanity of those needs: HRT, treatment for dysphoria, and some apportionment of community, if only in the manner of support networks for medical conditions.

(A friend survived cancer. Before the surviving was through, she went to groups with ‘other cancer people,’ she called them. When I was twelve, I developed ankylosing spondylitis. I subscribe to groups online for people who went through similar things. Very few think to be grateful outsiders do not call the first ‘a religious community for masochists who seek out chemotherapy’ or the second ‘a religious community for people who refuse to bend their spines,’ but I do and am grateful.)

In any case, maleness or Mongolianness? Swerving from Orientalism to general racialised deauthorising and overwriting, Francisco J. Galarte writes on the negated right to self-identification and self-report generally at play in Mexico’s constructions of gender, writing (Brown Trans Figurations, 2021):

Mexican women themselves, trans or not, are already kind of conceived as excessive and prone to deception in the sense that, in Mexican-american culture, women themselves are suspected of deception if they are not good mothers, if they are not good women.

The authority of racialised people to name and describe their own embodiments/bodies is systemically foreclosed, which system and process of foreclosure (such as in the Mexican-american context Galarte describes) does not vanish at the intracommunal threshold. Galarte, elsewhere and, at the very least, in interview with Tuck Woodstock, constructs a defence from pushback against his frequent use of the term, ‘Latinx,’ as such: “I think it is transphobia and homophobia hiding under the guise of a racial politic. … and that kind of position is, to me, all about heteropatriarchy and masculinist really kind of messed up toxic masculinity.” As such a politic may obscure the authorities of women in general, so it risks burying queer, trans, disabled, and other advocacies in urgent need of expression (read: urgent physical danger, be it political, medical, or otherwise). Watch as Muchi tries to stop up her yearning to belong:

Those clones, like near everybody else, scanned her eyes with the same needling longing to be loved, approved, validated: told they were real. In each of them – with no apparent tension or difficulty – lived that caring, that interest in life, the world, which she could never equal.

Of course, she bears the same longing. The paradox is plain. If the community of other clones does not satisfy, is it because their bleeding hearts repulse her? Or, is it that she cannot give what they need? That she cannot see the clones well enough to welcome them, as her community lies elsewhere? Hear again:

It was as though she were not really there – indeed had no power to be. Yichen craved her presence, though he already had it, and dreaded her absence, not as something yet to come, but as something ongoing, a condition of being he must not think on overlong, a condition altogether unmarred by her reality, in flesh, before him. Rather, if anything, her absence – the problem of her absence – was worsened by her presence. There. The moving touchable shape of Muchi, contoured by the same reddish-yellow ceiling lamps as his own real shape, the real veins in his hands, the real plate of gnocchi, the real shiraz.

And again:

“Is it possible,” said Muchi, “to give up your humanity without meaning to, or to never be given it in the first place?”

“Every sort of thing goes wrong. Don’t worry about “meaning to” or “wilfully.” I think however that happens to somebody, it’s the same thing. From what I’ve seen.”

“Do you, then,” taking advantage of the insanity he assigned her, “see it in me too?”

“Sorry, Muchi. Or Tamara. Which?”

“I don’t know. Any.”

“I don’t see you at all.”

Horse Breeders tells a story about the mundane occluded by spectacles and spectacularities. At last, the flourish is as such: Muchi’s spectacular characteristics (clone/Mongolian/emperor) occlude her mundane characteristics, one of which transness is not. The surrounding spectacle forces it out of mundanity, forces it to occlude the very trait that depends on it: i.e., womanhood. Every secondary character in Horse Breeders is a man. She lacks friendship with other women. She might find such with trans people, but connection through transness is secondary to the felt-need to belong with women in general, a thing for which she cannot, for the mundanity of it, find expression. (How, still further, does one find expression sufficient to speak from without speaking about? From but not about womanhood? From but not about transness? ….) …

“The closest medical centre?” asks the clowncierge (pronoun badge: honk/honks). Honk twists honks lips. “The closest’s a short clownbus to the edge of the ClownBD. But…,” honk scans you up and clown, “…the centre’s for women only.” Honk honks.

Cisness

2026

Awareness of difference is dangerous. If you grew up poor, yet somehow lived a life such that you are now reading this, you’re doubtless intimate with the perils of letting wealthy people catch sight of that poverty. Too great a fixation on difference leads to calculuses like: Mark spends ten thousand dollars on car-maintenance per year. A car would improve Rachel’s quality of life, but surgery even more; she’s been saving eight years, but emergencies, debilitations imposed by said lack of surgery, all mean she’s at around $5,305. A fifth of the way. Mark is a Christian, the humble kind. His church isn’t actively transphobic, but rather, avoids the subject. What would Mark’s pastor say if asked for advice? Who can guess why Rachel needs needs needs Mark never to hear of any of this? How does it feel to be cis? Awareness is dangerous. Rache is not happy with me for writing this. Survival depends on keeping her head down. Her and my oppressors’ peace of mind depends on it, too. How does it feel to be cis? No one knows. But does that mean cisness is not felt or feels like nothing? The framing is off. Instead: How does cisness feel, being you? What’s it like to be a bat? What’s it like to be cis? Is it good to be a god? Any good at all? At this site of confusion about the categorical threshold of cis-versus-trans, at this speculative juncture, help me interrogate realism as cissexually constructed. What is cis realism? What is cis speculative fiction? The short answer: that which does not feel trans is cis; that which does not feel speculative is realist. Trans and speculative they may be, ontically, but ontologically, they are cis and they are realist. This is contradictory, but comfortably so: reality and reality’s alternatives are expressed in a contradiction-tolerant lexicon. For a longer, more exacting answer, with a much-beloved quote from cis-realist masterpiece, The Silence of the Lambs:

Clarice: There’s no correlation in the literature between transsexualism and violence. Transsexuals are very passive.

Hannibal: …Billy is not a real transsexual. There are three major centers for transsexual surgery. I wouldn’t be surprised if Billy had applied for sex reassignment at one or all of them and been rejected… Billy hates his own identity, you see, and he thinks that makes him a transsexual.

(Impressions of Jodie Foster’s line go miles at the function.) Interestingly, for a trans woman to appear in realist fiction often serves to anchor that work’s realism, ground it even as gritty realism. The more demeaning and violent her representation (i.e. the less representative and less real), the more potent the realist signification for the cis audience. Much of popular TV’s hostility toward trans women is wilful, but none of that hostility is interesting. Unintended cruelty, though, is fertile for criticism. Enough has been said about Transparent and its errors, and in any case, nothing in that show is so egregious (in my opinion) as a representation in German time-travel drama, Dark. (Renowned for a website with the best UX-design of any TV show, ever.) The show’s first timeline/setting hosts a transfem character named Bernadette. Of course, her deadname is Benjamin and her nickname ‘Benni.’ She is played by a cis man. She sells sex out of a rust-holed trailer in a carpark. Her HRT is smuggled and dead-dropped in a forest by a teenage girl with drug links. The year is 2019. The place, central Germany. That a married man uses her to cheat is evidence of the man’s homosexuality. Other named characters have three actors, whereas Bernadette has one, appearing as neither her younger nor older self. Past and future are denied. She exists only when perceived. Her appearance – in Season Two – accompanies a revelation that the setting is one of two ‘dark timelines.’ But, there is no geopolitical or technological distinction between the setting and the real 2019. Dark bills itself as a realist drama, the science-fictional elements emergent to reinforce that realism and ground the setting in a real and really felt historical point. 2019 augurs an apocalyptic event (a climate change analogy, curiously realised in the COVID lockdowns) that originates in 1929, take shape in 1959, and by inaction, is enabled in 1989. The protagonists reside in each of these moments, each complicit in the coming catastrophe. See? It’s a metaphor. It grounds political realities in emotional realities. The macrosocial arena of history becomes a site of recognisable urgency on the microsocial level of a few individuals’ drives. Like Dark’s multiversal time-travel, Bernadette is a science-fictional element brought in to support the show’s realism. She – like the moss-laden caves that carry Dark’s cast to other worlds and times – is another of the forest’s secrets, of Leal’s “breathing, palpitating mysteries that hide behind things.” When the timeline diverts to unholiness and error – to the dark alongside the path – then the trannies crawl out.1 We are at once adult content and a fantastical abstraction. That is, in the reality of the Cis, our existence is magical realist-in-itself, our presence sufficient (no elaboration required) to convey the grim, mystic frisson of magical realism. Among many, two more characters who repeat this trope are David Duchovny’s Denise from Twin Peaks or the Witch in Fernanda Melchor’s Hurricane Season (2020). Here, then, cis realism is shown to be an oxymoron: realism is cisness and vice versa. Already, to depart from or investigate transness is to engage with magic, and so, already also, to think about one’s own cisness is to contemplate the reality’s outmost contours – i.e. to contemplate transness. Convention demands one do this carefully, soberly, through the apologetic media of fiction or film, and overlay the shot with a grey or sour-yellow tint. Do you see the edges? Do you know where your cisness is? (That question goes for trans readers too.) In any case, don’t answer aloud. What’s left to teach about cisness, I think, is best taught oppositionally, i.e., by reference to transness.

False Cows

At most ten slices of mango would cup in the mazemaker’s hands. When the king’s wife sounded the inner bell and summoned him to the entry hall, so, Daedalus tried to explain, “Half’s blended already.”

“What fruit’s that?” Queen Pasiphaë’s long, sun-riven neck craned this way and that, dogstooth cape – forked like a hummingbird’s tongue – de-buckled and hooked, gently so, between two bulges of unclaimed or forgotten coats.

“Mango,” Daedalus said.

She flinched. “What size is the blender? Can’t you slice it?”

“It’s sliced.”

“It won’t fit?”

“Let me show you.” Daedalus guided Pasiphaë to the kitchen. She watched, therein, as – from a marble cutting-board atop a marble benchtop – he heaped and lifted ten slices of mango, dropped them in a blender, and dropped another six. “Tell a lie,” he said. “Less than half.”

“But all now?”

“The lot.” The fruit whirred and no one spoke, bobbed heads in time with the blending – as to showtunes way away, far from the haze and grind.

“Now?”

Daedalus bobbed again, shifted the blended contents to a moss-yellow Nespresso tumbler, nodded and bobbed.

She asked, “Confidentiality’s real? You talk to family? Nothing’s slipped? Ever?”

Striding out the kitchen – into the open plan – and depositing himself to the cushioned middle, copper-coloured hair, lips like pomegranate ripe for mashing, tumbler gilded by one massive, ceiling-high blast of sunbeam, the mazemaker needed more. “Slipped out?”

“To the people closest. A boyfriend?” She joined him in the cushions, grey irises washed to invisible in the light. “Someone you break confidentiality with.”

“I’m not into men.”

“Really?”

“No,” Daedalus said. “When I was young, I was. But I hadn’t gone professional. I don’t talk.”

Pasiphaë’s gut unbraced, flattened. She stretched, let herself bask in the clean interior sunlight. “Then possibly.”

“Nothing gets out.”

“I need to fuck a bull. I need to trick a bull. I want to trick a bull so he fucks me,” she said.

Daedalus nodded.

“A specific bull.”

Daedalus rifled the cushions for a tablet. Found it. Typed as the customer spoke.

“This bull’s white. All day long he paces by the Salt Cliff, and now and now he glances at the drop. Where he paces at night, I can’t tell. He moves every way and the eye can’t hold. The darkness folds my bull’s angles and there’s not a splash of white left. I love him.”

Daedalus’s tablet tilted side-to-side, up-and-up as he contemplated the schematic, the sketches made, measurements to come. “It’s doable,” he said. “Diameters changed?”

“Same as ever.”

“Then, before we talk fee, why fucking? Why not speaking?”

Pasiphaë turned to the brightness, eyes shaded in the glare. In that light she said, “He doesn’t want that.”

Daedalus squinted. Ached to see past the veil. Discern the vortex. How could she know? Love. The occult comms-channel. Still its mechanism eluded him. No need for speaking. But how? He produced a number and showed Pasiphaë.

“That figure’s an ill portent,” she said.

“It’s the figure it is.”

“Can’t go lower?”

“No. Nor higher.”

“Nor higher? It goes higher. Add a one at the start.”

Daedalus flipped the tablet and stared. Business was slow and, like, how could he possibly make her understand? He added a plus and typed another million. The same figure, and one. Not turning the screen, he said, “Happy with that.”

“How long am I waiting?”

Ontology, Reality, Ontic & Magic(al) Realism & Secular Magical Realism

Ontology

The margins of this term are contested, and though efforts toward a specific and informed application of the word are made in each case, if one takes it to mean “all that is, as opposed to all that is not,” one will almost always appreciate the intent of the sentence in which “ontology” appears.

Reality

The fantastical world in which most nonfiction and all non-speculative fiction (of which realist fiction is an example) is set. Here, it is differentiated from the Real in the same way the terms are differentiated in Heidegger: “Reality (not the Real) is dependent upon care” (1927, 255). Fundamental ontology (clarified ahead) may be described as the gestalt of the Ontic (also clarified ahead) and Reality, and, as such, Reality may be described as that aspect/those aspects of fundamental ontology which are not ontic.

Ontic

The ontic is the pre-relational Real, a subset (as such) of fundamental ontology comprised of occurrences, processes, “beingnesses” a priori intelligibility, i.e., things (as such) before they are called “things” or are summoned into relationality, which relationality may be “innate”/an actuality awaiting mere observation or a byproduct of phenomenological interpretation/the output of a system/process later referred to as “hermeneutics.” When/insofar-as these “things” are summoned into relationality, they are (re)classified as Reality and declassified/no longer classifiable as ontic.

Let’s start with fable. With regard to their origins and creative trajectories, the arcs of magical realism are unlike those of fable (a supercategory into which Roberts Scholes, author of The Fabulators, sorts science fiction along with fantasy). Yet, despite their frequent assortment to science fiction, many works I deem “secular magical realism” lack the extra-realist departures and returns that define Scholes’ fables. Maggie Ann Bowers writes (28, 2004):

The science fiction narrative’s distinct difference from magical realism is that it is set in a world different from any known reality and its realism resides in the fact that we can recognize it as a possibility for our future. Unlike magical realism, it does not have a realistic setting that is recognizable in relation to any past or present reality.

In secular magical realism, unlike in Bowers’ science fiction, any effort to “recognise in relation” (relation, specifically, to a “past or present reality”) is a damned errand. Elsewhere, to distinguish magical realism from surrealism (Bowers, 22):

The extraordinary in magical realism is rarely presented in the form of a dream or a psychological experience because to do so takes the magic out of recognizable material reality and places it into the little understood world of the imagination. The ordinariness of magical realism’s magic relies on its accepted and unquestioned position in tangible and material reality.

It is this tension that sits my proposed sub-genre (secular magical realism) in the broadchurch of magical realism, even if it does not sit easily. To the secular magical realist, there are anxieties already and immediate to each of these words: “recognisable,” “material,” and “reality.” Robert Scholes must himself, in defining fables as “fiction[s] that offer us a world clearly and radically discontinuous from the one we know,” stake and limit his claims with that little modifier, “the one we know.” What world? Known to who? The shortcoming of these definitions, for Bowers and Scholes, is their presumption of a retrievable and illimitable real – and indeed, and most importantly, a real known both to them and the critics they reference. That Bowers’ future, or rather “possibilit[ies] for” – quote – “our future,” can bear no recognisable relation to whatever this known real/recognisable world may be says more about the imagined reality in question – and the ideological assumptions of the critics – than about science fiction or futurological literature. In Dick’s example, which certainly should be called secular magical realist (if deserving of its own, esoteric qualifications), there is a manner of magic which is indeed ordinary and that ordinariness does “rely on its accepted and unquestioned position in tangible and material reality,” as the magic of Bowers’ magical realism must do.

Despite territorial shifts and re-originations, critics of magical realism have, in general, felt an uncomplicated happiness in citing one remark from Franz Roh, the German critic and photographer who coined the term “magischer Realismus”[^3] in 1925. Roh, as translated by Davos Frauenkirch (1925): “with the word "magic," as opposed to "mystic," I wish to indicate that the mystery does not descend to the represented world, but rather hides and palpitates behind it.” Luis Leal returns to this quote as a near-definitive description of magical realism, in one case as the genre’s distinguishing trait versus fantastic literature, i.e., “in … magical realist works the author does not need to justify the mystery of events, as the fantastic writer has to. In fantastic literature the supernatural invades a world ruled by reason. In magical realism”the mystery does not descend to the represented world, but rather hides and palpitates behind it”” (123, 1967). In another, he bonds Roh’s explanation to a definition by Arturo Uslar Pietri (120): “there was the consideration of man as a mystery surrounded by realistic facts. A poetic prediction or a poetic denial of reality. What for lack of another name could be called a magical realism.)” From these sources, thus, Leal develops the following distinction and definition: “The magical realist does not try to copy the surrounding reality (as the realists did) or to wound it (as the Surrealists did) but to seize the mystery that breathes behind things” (123), and, in interview with a biographer, has said, “If you can explain it, then it's not magical realism. Without thinking of the concept of magical realism, each writer gives expression to a reality he observes in the people. To me, magical realism is an attitude on the part of the characters in the novel toward the world” (127-128, García 2000). It is that last note which gives the widest, most navigable access for secular magical realism as a subgenre of magical realism. Only, even here there is difference, thus the ‘secular’ modifier. The secular magical realist does not ‘seize’ the “hiding, palpitating mysteries that breathe behind things”: they are, the author is, those mysteries, and where their breath is felt, there is the art and the object of secular magical realism.

Magical realism usually refers to fiction in which one or both of these elements are present: i. fantastical detours which serve as revisitations of, and support for claims about, Reality; ii. magic that is not strange to the author’s world-schema, i.e., magic that is “real.” To give some precision to “secular magical realism,” then, we can define it (for now) as an elaboration of the second element, i.e., magic that is “real.” In this element, the magic (in the context of the artwork) is acquired as an “other” even as it returns and attaches to Reality. Acquired, from this ‘othered magic,’ are arcs, themes, resonances, titbits from folklore, superstition, ancient and traditional storytelling, religion, i.e., from social and cultural sources. The magic is a found (or given) property that may be instrumentalised toward egressions from and intercessions on Reality; that is not of the Real, i.e, but may serve as an access to the Real. In secular magical realism, the magic precedes and is greater than Reality: the origin of the text’s magic is the author’s biological ontology. It is not acquired and neither can it be turned down. Far more often than not (this is not part of the definition, but its truth might be clarifying of the genre’s situation), magic is something the secular magical realist encounters first as a problem, a barrier to articulation instead of something ‘brought in’ to aid articulation. Whereas magical realism that is not secular draws its magic from cultural and folkloric sources, secular magical realism draws its magic (though it does not so much draw on magic as depend on it, problematically, for life) innately, from being-itself. The oneiropomp of the secular magic cosmos is not one’s guide to vaster worlds, but a tongue-tied mutterer trying to speak itself into the waking world: though the general category of “author” is oft accused of oneiropomposity, the secular magical realist is unique in its reversal of the oneiropompous motion, i.e., its labour is in summoning creatures back from, or leading them out of, the dream, among which creatures the author includes themself. Most works of secular magical realism do not depend in totum on secular magic, their sources being many and, typically, greatly varied (that erratic variation is itself a tendency of the genre), but it might aid illustration to gesture toward three distinct works of magical realism. I will start with one in which the magic is strictly non-secular, then move to a novel in which the magic is varied, and in the third example (a work that has not previously been referred to as “magical realist”), the magic will be strictly secular.

For the first: Life and Death are Wearing Me Out is a novel by Mo Yan (2008), in which a baby recounts the experiences and events of six reincarnations as first a landlord, then several animals. Magic’s role as something acquired and used to articulative advantage – acquired from cultural sources, particularly – is evident here. For a variable text: the novel, Transparent City (Ondjaki, 2012), deals with the aftermath of the Angolan Civil War, as the protagonist’s body becomes weightless in parallel with his divestment from agency and power, whereas other bodies increase in weight, the shifting physics of the setting resulting in the setting’s explosive destruction as corruption is given its own thermodynamic/physical manifestation. The magics are direct and, in the sense of their directness and creative strategy, are brute-force expressions of the author’s experiences and the social, emotional, ethical tolls of the war, and in that respect, they are secular. As a quotable example of magic encountered as a problem, the novel also gives us this (209):

«The war», it was said, «is a bleeding memory, and at any moment you open your mouth, or gesticulate, and what comes out is an incarnate trace of things you didn't know you knew» all Angolans had, therefore, some paranoia with weapons or armaments, all had a story to tell or an episode to invent

Here, in secular fashion, a form is given to the articulative complaint of secular magic. Still, there are culturally and conventionally derivative qualities in the novel’s informed use of magical realist tropes, the ensouled essence of the protagonist which remains after his body’s dissipation, and the use of apocalypse-thematics. Such blends are, it is important to note, however, characteristic of almost all works of secular magic realism. The third example, the strictly secular text, is Ralph Ellison’s Invisible Man (1952).

It is a little disingenuous, I should say right now, to describe any work of secular magical realism as totally secular, as social and cultural residues come unbidden and will inform any articulation one makes, even be that the articulation of an invisible magic from without the social and cultural real. But, the word “strict” should leave enough wiggle-room and not commit me to implying “total.” There are two instances of magic to which one might point in Invisible Man, the first being the titular invisibility, which is metaphorical but attains, at last, to a kind of physicality in the narrator’s imprisonment underground, the narrational voice/narrator defending his status as a mere textual object by asking, in the novel’s closing paragraph, “Being invisible and without substance, a disembodied voice, as it were, what else could I do?” The new literalness of the narrator’s invisibility overthrows its metaphorical aspect and bonds itself with the author’s own invisibility, which can now be understood as more than metaphor, as Real, because here the author is, here he is, writing as a disembodied voice because after all “what else could I do?” At the novel’s close, too, the narrator ponders a return, out from their underground enclosure to the world above, an outward reentry from the Real to Reality, so that he might make interventions upon it once again, invisible or not. His means of escape from that underground enclosure is not explained, but in the pairing of this voice with the author’s (with Ellison’s) we may understand the text itself as that extrusion from the Real, the feat-itself of magical escape. In its final arrival at surrealism – at a paradoxical yet realist conclusion – the novel cuts an access into the waking real through which the oneiropomp-author may step, and does step. Cultural investitures inform the mechanism of this magic only in marginal, even trivial, respects. The incision is exact to its task and derived wholly of secular methods – and is no less magical for it.

So, we return to the oneiropomp, the author whose magical writing draws from the body-itself, and who thus writes magic toward the incorporation of – or at least articulation of – their body, their selfhood, their biological ontology to a Reality that is otherwise uncomprehending of it. They are each of them (begging this modifier not be equated with “totally”) strictly secular applications of magical realism.

It was her right, of course – it was given to others – to come to this revelation at her own pace, to see it after years perhaps, after finding the path to disillusionment on her own. Her girlfriend’s ugliness – fine – should have been there, but at the back, not the forefront of her mind. An insurance. An insurance by which to gain peace when the affection ebbed away, peace in the discovery that it was not really so valuable, what she was giving up, and that, in fact, there was wisdom in giving it up: because, through that new-gained perception, she could recognise and believe her own growth, trust her newfound confidence. She was better at seeing now, proven by the embarrassment of having not seen before: so it would go. And in that embarrassment would be the whole, great comfort. But now, she was being made to see it far too early, long before any feeling dropped away, and would have to live directly, without deniability, through the problem of her perception, her advance-awareness of that being the cause and the origin of her eventual loss of love: her girlfriend’s inarguable, un-unseeable ugliness. So it was that, by making her see it, it was as if Josephine had broken up with her herself, but by some evil fait accompli such that it now had to be her, Variance, who took responsibility: as if she had checkmated her, put her in a situation where, now that she could not deny or stop seeing that she was ugly, she was now going to have to initiate the break, but with no plausible deniability, no means of sparing feelings. It was as if, without Variance having done anything, Josephine had made her into an evil person, and yet done so correctly, using nothing but the truth. She could not torment her by remaining with her either, considering what she now saw, so the only way to not be evil, like a Nazi given his orders, was to kill herself. She debated, then: kill herself, or end a relationship on the understanding that she had been wrong, young, confused by the light, and actually her girlfriend was just a lot uglier than she previously realised, with no realistic hope of pitching it otherwise. Kill herself, or that. Even if she chose the latter, she might just kill herself anyway, afterward. At least, if she killed herself first, she would have manoeuvred out of her girlfriend’s checkmate, denied her the satisfaction, etc., etc.

She swung her legs over the side of the bed and sat, and stared, and stopped thinking in words.

Aeons ago, she read a book, a collection of short stories, and in the longest story, a sort of bildungsroman, there was a scene where the main kid – the kid’s dad bought him a “whore” for his 16th – met this person, this educated left-liberal from another, bigger city, who said sex work was rape. But that wasn’t what Variance was thinking about. She remembered the character’s spirals after that conversation. Rape and rape and what is rape? Spiralling. Starting to poke. A sayable sentence: the sex worker’s situation was more ambiguous than the kid’s. But the kid started poking, trying to dismiss the problem, rewind the spiral. He poked to convince himself the word was not applicable, but when he considered what words were, all preceded by adjectives like “statutory” and “sexual” and followed by “that my dad did to me,” the first word, “rape,” lodged only more firmly, felt only more true. She supposed it must be a kind of kink, variously a self-esteem thing or a betrayal for the sakes of betrayal: some people believed in beauty as an absolute principle, so when those people got bored and needed to kick down a crystal palace, etc., they fucked her. In Variance, it must be both. A limited confidence in her own appearance, plus a kind of worship of beauty, constant visits to the throne of it, an austere willingness to go without sex (except sex with Josephine, the repulsion of which, for her girlfriend, must be like jerking off with a rough glove on, a lonely masochism) for months if the options were not models. Kept her close for that purpose. She planned the best way to argue this, to prove to Variance that these were her thoughts, and with a mind to listing the key points in her notes, unlocked her phone. To make an exegesis of “Stamina,” and to develop and identify its situation within secular magical realism, this next section discusses its interaction with the thinning, tightening, flattening, ever-greying crust under your, my, everyone’s eyes. To re-summarise, the cataracts, the constellating soot-spot-holes burned in every light concern inverted expeditions: i.e. conversations with or journeys to an alternate reality/alterity that is, in some meaningful sense, vaster than the reality that encloses it. Examples given for comparison include four novels and two short stories, though 10:04, “Archipelagos,” and Beatrice the Sixteenth are paid only cursory reference. The cataracts’ texts, in the order of analysis, are Wild Geese by Soula Emmanuel (a novel which attempts to engage these subjugated interior alterities from a realist schema), “The Beatrix Gates” by Rachel Pollack, and Tell Me I’m Worthless by Alison Rumfitt.

By comparison with Wild Geese, the complexity of contested social “universes” and the realities and perceptions these provide for, as a narrative problem generally and, in the case of “Stamina” and Wild Geese both, a relationship-problem specifically, may be given new clarity by the tracing of the two works’ opposing trajectories. As will be elaborated, where Wild Geese recounts an attempt to guide another, to, as it were, encourage an inverted expedition, “Stamina” recounts the (mis)use of an enclosed exteriority to block off such an expedition – in essence, a malign use of liberation to suppress liberation. Bringing this clarification toward a recognition of conflict, the analysis then draws on “The Beatrix Gates.” In “The Beatrix Gates,” visions of magic alternate worlds or wondrous futures are summoned to elevate the range of what may be assumed possible, or hoped for. This is stated to be a continuation of the project implied in Irene Clyde’s authorship of Beatrice the Sixteenth. That revelation of hope through magic and alterity is distorted in “Stamina” to elicit notes of doom and condemnation, as its monstrous protagonist accesses these domains exactly to control/control for their partner’s hopes. These domains of hope, as such, are discussed as sites of plurality and are opposed to reality, as constructed and narrowed by cis hermeneutics, which discussion leads into a review of the similarity between “Stamina” and Tell Me I’m Worthless. Where Tell Me I’m Worthless reports on a forced extraction from reality (into a deranged, narrower-still, fascist alterity, or a paradoxical suspension to the limbo intervening the reality and the alterity), “Stamina” gives a near-parallel report, albeit one in which a liberating alterity is drawn on to further narrow/limit the already-extant possibilities (the powers, magics) of reality. Here, three narrative arcs common to the bile, the weeping, the emphysemic dripoff of my and my mother’s and her mother’s and the mother’s and her and her and her her the her infected lacrimal-sacs are assigned and, from the interaction of those, a meditation emerges on the haunting, as opposed to haunted, relationship of the trans author to realism.

The exegesis of “Stamina” will bring in an additional reference outside the four primary trace-sources for See us at the Winter Ball! 18 July at the District Villa! This year’s theme is Snowmen’s Heaven, Wild Geese by Soula Emmanuel (2023). In Wild Geese, an Irish trans woman nearing the completion of a PhD reunites with a former partner, a cis woman she last saw before she transitioned, and their brief reunion is recounted in a series of diary entries. The temptation to narrativise this encounter is not resisted, evidently, as the novel appears to be semi-autobiographical. Like Wild Geese, “Stamina” is written from the perspective of an author who uses a relationship as material for their fiction and, thus, strives to direct the relationship and the fiction along complementary trajectories. (Whereas the protagonist of Wild Geese does the same unwittingly and naïvely, the protagonist of “Stamina” does it knowingly and cynically). In both cases, the outcome of this attempt is the collapse of both the relationship and the art. Both protagonists draw on imagined futures and use them to intervene on material situations, in Wild Geese to support and advocate a vision of a greater, more expansive future, and in “Stamina” to limit scopes of possibility and restrict velocities that might reach “levels of reality” beyond the “social level.” In a narrowed and politicised sense, whereas Wild Geese demonstrates a progressive and pro-trans speculative device, “Stamina” exemplifies the use of a reactionary and transphobic speculative device.

When the trans woman narrator of Wild Geese re-enters a relationship with a cis woman, she (naïvely but therefore also, the author suggests, culpably) expects her partner to delineate the richer, more fertile dimension into which the narrator has travelled from that in which she resided during their previous relationship, i.e., before she transitioned. In this expectation, the narrator evokes and gestures toward hope, but it is a version of hope that necessarily includes hope for trans women. Entailed in this gesture is the abandonment of institutions her cis woman partner and, indeed, most cis people depend on for meaning. (Trans women would depend on these institutions, too, were they not denied them.) Emmanuel writes the complexity of this failed articulation, or failed attempt by the narrator to make her world comprehensible to her cis partner, as such (197):

I was suddenly struck by the troubling thought that I no longer had access to the thoughts that had propelled me back then. It was like I had been speaking a different language, strange impulses emerging from a past self as if from a tomb.

Here, Emannuel provides a concise summary of the communicative impasse between the constructions of reality – the “thoughts” – and vocabulary accessible within cis personhood and the constructions and vocabulary, the “language” one speaks, accessed beyond cis norms. What the narrator has seen in her ex’s absence, she cannot tell. The narrator tries to speak it, and her ex tries to understand, catching a glimmer of meaning here or there, but in the end cannot.

Thinking on glimmers of comprehension, the novel 10:04 by Ben Lerner comes to mind. In 10:04, Lerner seeks to reinvigorate hopes of a utopian future by the invocation of “utopian glimmers,” finding these glimmers in the midst of complex and (politically, ethically, and otherwise) compromised social phenomena. Just as Lerner’s hopes for utopia depend on glimmers of things to come, things evident only after scrutiny, such that to live for utopia one must live in flickered moments, so do trans women’s hopes for survival. Those glimmers are the only pockets of breathable air in “the real world,” at least as cis norms assign it, and the same is true in the realist world of Soula Emmanuel’s Wild Geese. Emmanuel recounts a failed attempt to guide a cis person beyond the consensuses and hermeneutics of cis reality, i.e., through something like the process of overcoming repression. Comparatively, “Stamina” describes a feat of linear violence, as the repressed trans masc protagonist (a monstrous protagonist, in a sense) seeks to enclose his partner socially and cut off the escapes and perspectival expansions that would normally accompany her coming out. Whereas Wild Geese dares its cis audience with the prospect of a more fulfilling and more robust sociality, the protagonist of “Stamina” seeks (quite literally) to drain his partner of her self-actualising potential and to use the energy of that potential to sublimate and prolong his own repression, toward an absolute subjection and overcoming of dysphoria through indefinite repression. The mechanism of the draining is outlined as such:

There was nothing left to take from her, her manfulness near-spent, unreplaced by femininity of course – that, let flourish, would bulwark against the extraction, his sympathetic pleasure whenever the masculinity spilled out of her, oozed and unwanted, her with nowhere to retreat, settling as a limp husk on the floor of their loungeroom every workday night.

These extracted potentials he expends and wastes in the authorship of a pulp science-fiction series, referenced as a secondary narrative in the frame of the short story. When trans authors engage this dilemma, this interplay of contested universes (i.e., that of cis norms/hermeneutics versus the All, the Real, fundamental ontology and the multisocial textures thereof), their plots and settings begin, unavoidably, to layer. Often, the author will control this complexity with frame narration.

The short story “The Beatrix Gates” by Rachel Pollack presents three narratives, each a component of the others, first a science-fiction setting in which an idealised future – a world ruled by trans people – is given as the frame for a fabulous secondary narrative (fabulous as in ‘of fable,’ though it is fabulous in every other sense, too). This secondary fable depicts an alternate reality in which everybody is either ‘a red’ or ‘a green’, until, at the story’s close, the trans green protagonist comes to envision vaster, more colourful realities. Here, in a third, nonfictional section, the author herself steps in to give something like an exegesis, developing to a meditation on hope and an independent essay. Even this section is, implicitly, a continuation and fulfilment of the initial frame: the science-fiction story closes on an elder being asked by a younger trans woman to “tell me of the Old World… Tell me how it changed, what it felt like” (188), and it is sensible to read the elder as Pollack herself – i.e., a tongue-in-cheek self-insert, aggrandised to the point of parody – as she relates to her readers. In this manner, Pollack embeds a secondary and then tertiary depth to her essay, allowing it to exist in and draw from the sources and materials of an extra-real, extra-cosmic discourse.

Rachel Pollack’s writing draws from something nearer to fundamental ontology (it opens a dialectic access thereto/from). Thereby, she admits hope into her writing’s purview, lends ballast to hope’s seriousness as a literary and political object. In Pollack, what’s more, hopes are amplified to the point of torment, extrapolated beyond the contemporary climate to arenas yet-to-come, the amplitude of her hopes for trans women, for example, pushing past survival and into triumph. She thus perpetuates the original, universe-slipping mechanism of trans belonging from Clyde’s Beatrice the Sixteenth, but continues its slip-motion into reality, so that dreamed-of alterities may now inform the vocabularies and discourses of realism. We can see how “Stamina” distorts this effect, when, at its conclusion, the sci-fi secondary narrative depicts an intervention by the protagonist’s alternate-universe cis self, who supersedes the trans masc protagonist and condemns the saga to return to its original setting. The monstrous protagonist’s action on/against his own writing is the control and vandalism of its potentials by using the narrow reality he lives in/depends on to delimit and ridicule any hopes of departure therefrom. The fallacy to which this technique leads is demonstrated by its elaboration ad absurdum, as given in the secondary narrative in dialogue between the trans masc protagonist (Success Maximum) and the cis woman alternate universe-self who kills and supersedes him (Suicide Mary):

“Truth doesn’t change across universes, and it’s true in mine. It’s a fact. In my universe, I am a fact.”

“Actually,” said Suicide, “truth does change across universes, and now, you’re in mine.”

“The people who destroyed my Fall exist. They’re out there. They… it… massacre… trillions… my people…”

“I’m sorry, Mr Maximum, but that’s just not the case.”

Here, consensus reality intrudes on the Real, interrupts the speculative mechanism as it gestures toward the Real, insisting on its univocity even as that univocity’s articulation depends on an assumption of plurality. “Those are not real. This is real.” Tell Me I’m Worthless invokes plurality and contradiction-tolerance too, albeit as a reactionary and suppressive force.

The narrative frame of Tell Me I’m Worthless is literal, as here the frames and framework of a reality-distorting, fascist house perform this operation. Surrealist and horrific, the critical dilemma of the novel’s primary narrative is how one might reconcile and retrieve a ‘real’ or usable account, a usable sense of self, usable principles, usable mechanisms of justice, etc. from one’s traversal of a pocket dimension in which the truth is different for all who cross. The dilemma is aggravated by the pocket dimension assigning its victims the most fascist version of the truth devisable. (The characters who enter the house are subject to violence at the hands of the other characters, in a manner accordant with common bigotries, fears, and stereotypes about the offending characters’ demographic backgrounds. Those who survive the house recall the reality in which they were the victim, and no other.) The framed, secondary narrative recounts the construction of the house, a construction ironically told as the substantiation of a form that in a sense always already was, an eventuation written into the blueprint of England, just as England was written – as a kind of dark or diabolic prophecy – into the blueprint of the world. However, the haunting of the house begins in a wife’s despair at her husband’s outing, in the early 19th century, as a pederast and homosexual. (It is for the latter he is jailed and for her own loss of reputation that his wife grieves.) Intent on demolishing it, she visits the incomplete frame of what was to become their marital home. A voice (the house’s voice) promises to teach her the secrets of conversion therapy and, at the revelation of those unknown secrets, she hangs herself. There are multiple secondary narratives concerning subsequent generations of victims, which include that of an accomplice who would lure victims to the house. Here, the interior vastness of England is given as a compromised and (almost comically) evil force, one that uses its aptness for relativity to assign singular, albeit conflicted realities to its subjects and forces them into opposition. It is a malign inversion of the secular magic realist effect.

Typically, the secular magical realist guide, the oneiropomp, opens accesses for new potentials and articulations inside reality, called from without/called from the multivocal Real. This is the exact device of Callum Angus’s “Archipelagos.” In “Archipelagos,” two children disappear from known reality, sailing onto an infinitely vast, yet paradoxically inland ocean, which – by force of their transness or some unnamed, equivalent, inner sympathy – only they can see. Voyages both liberating and annihilating, bearing one to a vast, inner exteriority, are typical of secular magic realism. In Tell Me I’m Worthless, however, a monstrous inversion of this takes place, in which even the meagre range of multivocity at hand to consensus reality is stolen from the protagonists. A malevolent oneiropomp swallows the novel’s victims into its exterior depths and, by a genius consciousness of alternate and multidimensional truths, invents the ugliest truth conceivable, a truth from which they cannot tear their eyes, such that they will never see another, and spits them back out, up to the inner flesh of England with none but that truth in their minds, none but that evil, solitary light to guide by. Rumfitt’s invention here, i.e. the House of Tell Me I’m Worthless, though an inversion of secular magical realism, illustrates the devastating potential of the genre’s conceit when used for ill, and so makes the potency and merits of its effect more imaginable, both for the existing audience and – as an unrealised potential; if the genre’s situation and conceit were better articulated, better understood, and better known – for anybody else.

The universe of “Stamina” – the near-realist frame setting, i.e. – does also depend on a speculative principle, which is, “What if it were possible to repress to the point of actual, achieved conversion – and, if it were possible, by what magic would it be so?” As such, the “reality” of the setting contains this possibility: here, one really can repress and, by that repression, overcome one’s own transness. The mechanism/magic is named only ambiguously, but also, the peculiar fantasy of the story is the strictness of its realism, an allegiance to a cisnormative mode of realism that is necessarily – obviously so, when announced by a trans voice – also a speculative mode, even in this case an absurd mode. A straightforward juxtaposition tells us this: think of a cis person saying, “Here is my story about a trans person who overcomes dysphoria by repressing it,” and now think of a trans person saying the same. Who the speaker is determines whether the story is plausibly realist (as in the former) or necessarily speculative (the latter) – but the former voice is the arbiter of realism.

For trans authors, to write toward realism is a speculative motion and, therefore, to write from one’s own standpoint, to not move at all, is to face accusations of “groundbreaking alterity, brilliant albeit unfocused experimentation, boundary-smashing, journey-making.” As the usurped cis captain of “Stamina”’s secondary narrative tells it, “there are uncountably many cosmoses,” and outside the confining privileges of reality (as bastion, as a thing constructed by cis hermeneutics) there are uncountably many would-be realities which must necessarily express themselves as fancies, fabulations, and speculations. Call these would-be realities “sites of ontology.” The conceit of both “Stamina” and Tell Me I’m Worthless is to recount a more hostile, more violent site of ontology than that/those from which the presently dominant, hegemonic reality has emerged. Two forces of reaction engulf the multivocal flows of a semantic reality – to which they stand at an external relation – as it threatens to expand/pluralise/grow more inclusive, and there, at that site of monstrous exteriority, digest them and, eventually, regurgitate corrupted, compromised versions of the new subjectivities the semantic reality had stood to include. Sam re-envisions his girlfriend, Josephine, as a man and assigns her a flawed, conditional personhood, her self-expression dependent on his, as the speculative device he creates (the saga he writes) assigns a new public and discursive context in which Josephine must then reside. (The saga assigns that context through its entanglement with discourses of trans femininity.)

This is the discursive environment into which the protagonist of “Stamina” is writing, despite his tangential and superficial awareness of it. Various mechanisms (some of them magical) of transmisogyny are also described as he deploys them against Josephine. Whereas, in “Stamina,” evil triumphs by force of magic – by the dark magics of a trans masc hermeneutic scaffolded into and given to the reinforcement to cis hermeneutics – in Tell Me I’m Worthless the opposite tendency emerges. One of its three perspective-characters (a repressed trans man and aggressive TERF; the others are a trans woman and the House) breaks through the confines of the aberrant, fascist reality imposed by the House and, so, overcomes his repression and transitions. Extra-realist sites of ontology haunt every realist narrative, but the complexity for trans authorship is that the author may never approach realism as one who is merely haunted, but must come to/write it as one who haunts, whose work is haunting, and who is no less haunted for it.

Works within the ash, the little that’s left are so categorised for their enactment of an inverted expedition. Much as Tell Me I’m Worthless does, “Stamina” further inverts this expedition, doubling back to deepen and reinforce the setting’s limits and boundaries – begins a voyage into an inner wondrousness only in order to eradicate it. Compared with Wild Geese, the short story recounts the success of a transphobic and reactionary creative device, the complexity of its success given a starkness by consideration of Emmanuel’s description, in Wild Geese, of a pro-trans and progressive creative device’s argumentative failure. (One might reflect that this, in trans literature, is a far more common story: it is easier to write a convincing victory for a transphobic protagonist than a trans one.) Pollack’s “The Beatrix Gates” collapses fable, science fiction, and auto-theory to build a new (i.e. previously unthinkable) narrative of hope with a definite focus on trans liberation and the richness of trans imagination. She centres this around plurality or a capacity to stand at a point of relational limbo or outsideness. It is this capacity that is developed into a malign force in “Stamina” as the protagonist writes – from a site of trans imagination – toward a reduction of possible realities, i.e., draws on a multivocal Real to buttress a univocal reality. As said, the nearest effect to this, among the compared works, is that of Tell Me I’m Worthless. In “Stamina,” Schiz (I want to use some Deleuzean jargon) is tooled toward the return and solidification of the protagonist’s purchase on paranoia (i.e., the Oedipal bastion). In Tell Me I’m Worthless, the effect is the suspension of its protagonists to a site of schizophrenic-paranoiac limbo. In review of all above, three key modes or arcs of inverted expedition are described, which bear re-listing:

Organism Writing or Queer Theory’s Failure

Methodologies for Trans Artmaking

2026

The art and research methods of False Cows derive from the methodological traditions of queer art and theory at the same time as they conflict with, and seek to grow by the vandalisation of, queer art and theory. As a project, False Cows depends on the emergence and invention of new methodologies appropriate to trans theory, as distinct from those of queer theory. To that effect, this methodological analysis provides an account of the utility various methods from queer and feminist theory hold for the thesis alongside the limits of those methods, closing on an extended report of a conflict that has arisen between trans and queer theorists. As the essay builds toward that report, it cites extensively from Andrea Long Chu’s dialogue with Emmett Harsin Drager in TSQ, i.e., “After Trans Studies.” (This dialogue serves as a reliable complicator for, and rhetorical counter to, many methodological prescriptions from queer and feminist theory.) Opening with a description of the thesis’s overall method, i.e. fictocriticism as fictoanalysis (per Anna Gibbs’ and my own writings), I then move to a gloss of The Anxiety of Authorship, a celebrated work of feminist literary theory, and considers that text’s own fraught role within queer theory. This consideration develops to a discussion of queer theory’s own comparable limits for and troubled interactions with trans theory, a discussion centred around Jack Halberstam’s The Queer Art of Failure. Here, the two fields are played off each other and their mutual conflict is given some emotive and argumentative shape. Trans theory’s subordinate relationship to queer theory is shown as a relationship it must rescue itself from, a claim supported by an elaboration on Chu’s and Halberstam’s conflict. How queer theory may nonetheless benefit trans theory – and trans art – despite this relational conflict is the question then asked. (Note: Here, I think the art and theory of marginalised communities as necessarily bonded, each as attempts toward a regained or advanced articulability, such that in advocacy for marginalised voices there can be no theory without practice. (Still, I avoid writing “praxis” because i. the historical baggage of that word might be overwhelming and ii. the English translation, “process,” recurs enough here that, set beside each other, the boundary might get confusing.)) In answer, Anna Gibbs, i.e. the fictocritical method advocated by her and developed as fictoanalysis by me, is revisited, described further, and advocated anew. In the end, it is decided that trans art – when it deigns to draw from queer theory at all – must draw from queer theory’s failures.

False Cows advances the fictocritical method of “writing as research,” deploying the model described by my own Master’s thesis, i.e. fictoanalysis (a method of fictocriticism), alongside the shifting models/anti-models and reinventions of fictocriticism’s other methods. “Fictocriticism is a way of writing for which there is no blueprint and which must be constantly invented anew,” writes Anna Gibbs (2005). To give a quick summary of fictocriticism’s overall affect, Anna Gibbs refers to it as “writing as research,” and as writing that “insist[s] on the necessity of a certain process.” Further, Gibbs describes this process “a hit and run guerilla action, tactical rather than strategic. A precise intervention into a specific situation” (ibid.). In a retrospective on Anna Gibbs’ and others’ fictocritical authorship through the 90s, Alison Bartlett finds traces of fictocriticism’s affect in Helene Cixous’s écriture féminine, following these traces through to the work of Luce Irigaray, specifically, the essay, “When our lips Speak Together” (1980). Regarding attempts to teach this essay to students, Bartlett writes the following (2006, “Dear Regina”):

When I explained [Irigaray’s] challenge to psychoanalysis, that the entire theory of subject formation and the subject’s relation to language would be inexorably changed if it took women’s sexuality as its metaphorical model rather than male sexuality, they were silent. So I just kept talking about her exhortations to invent a new language, to find our body’s language, and the ingenious wordplay between labial lips and oral lips, between sexuality and language, between women speaking together and addressing each other as lovers, as lesbian lovers.

Irigaray’s and Bartlett’s conceit here, i.e. taking a different model, is common to fictocritical writing and marginalised literatures, whether the conceit is toward original analyses or original creative effects. Extending this Irigarayan trace, Bartlett characterises fictocriticism, somewhat reductively, as “‘straight’ academic writing [that] provides rationale for validating other kinds of writing which are embodied, indebted, partially located in [the writer’s] theoretical enterprise” (ibid.) She further situates the terminology within an Australian-exclusive context while generalising its identity to match that of “theory-fiction, paracriticism, postcriticism, metafiction, [and] ‘anecdotal theory’” (ibid.), while levelling subtle criticism toward Anna Gibbs’ notion of fictocriticism as “non-academic writing,” quipping, “Most of the writers [who Gibbs published as fictocritics] are now in academia. I guess that interest in continental psychoanalytic philosophy put them in good stead.” (Here, at least, Bartlett’s jibe is aptly made.) Returning to Gibbs nonetheless enables us to invoke a description of the method’s utility as a discrete ‘strategy,’ rather than Bartlett’s generalised, international model (i.e., a description of the likely effects of its openness and research-based/processual approach to storytelling), follows as such (Gibbs):

What I'm suggesting, then, is that writing may be driven as much by the body as by thought: it partakes not simply of ideas, but of sensory and affective knowledges which are not secondary to thought (its window-dressing), but which are active in deciding not only the forms ideas will take, but also in discovering or inventing ideas themselves.

In response to a call by Marguerite Duras to “move on to the rhetoric of women, one that is anchored in the organism, in the body,” Gibbs gives this concise, easily transferrable suggestion of how Duras’ call for a rhetoric of women may be read, too, as a call to fictocritical writing:

We might read this as a call to see what happens when epistemology takes the female rather than the male body as its starting point (as others have wondered, say, what would happen if we took smell rather than vision as a starting point for epistemological imaginings).

Incredibly helpfully, and amusingly in this context, Gibbs quickly gives the following elaboration:

But today - without forgetting either the reality of sexual difference or the complexities of gender as it fails to map neatly onto it (as in transsexualism, the tomboy experience, the girlie gay boy or any other number of lived experiences to which one could point) - we might instead read Duras as speaking advisedly of ‘the [sic [sic]] organism’.

The revision for the purposes of this thesis is to see what happens when epistemology takes the trans rather than the cis body as its starting point. The answer is: Epistemology becomes a genre of speculative fiction, albeit one that writes toward an end to or escape from speculation. Usually, this regard for the trans body as “un-neat” and “complex” and as something which stands at a media threshold between a well-modelled site of sense, linearity, and authority and an unsituated ruin of anti-modality, postmodernism, and crisis— usually, to hold such a regard and to use trans bodies toward this discursive effect is a feat of bigotry and simple othering, as is obvious after a moment’s reflection. (Note: Most of the diction in the previous sentence is lifted directly or as simile from Anna Gibbs’ passage about trans and gender-non-confirming epistemologies.) But – not to give Anna Gibbs the benefit of the doubt (I don’t; I never do for cis people) – she has, at the very least, on this occasion, gotten lucky. Even though she writes the trans body as an antihuman force which “collapses” the “distinction between discourse and metadiscourse,” and as something which, upon exposure to it, opens the [unwritten-as-such, but necessarily cis] writer to an ontic exterior, she does however stick the landing.

The site Anna Gibbs names, “the [sic [sic]] organism,” is indeed the trans home world. It is the privilege of a cis narrational perspective to travel to and return from it, and it is the ancient tragedy of the trans narrational perspective to be forever bound and struggling to escape from it. Lending some emotive and historical ballast to this difference, Andrea Long Chu gives the following in “After Trans Studies,” which it is worth quoting at length:

…the transsexual is the only thing that trans can describe that queer can't. The transsexual is not queer; this is the best thing about her. Take Agnes, the pseudonymous transsexual woman who famously posed as intersex at UCLA's Gender Identity Clinic in the late fifties in order to obtain access to vaginoplasty. Agnes's case was chronicled by Harold Garfinkel (1967 2006) in an article that's now taught in trans studies courses. (It's the sixth entry in The Transgender Studies Reader.) Agnes is regularly celebrated as some kind of gender ninja: savvy, tactical, carefully conning the medical-industrial complex into giving her what she wants (see, e.g., Preciado 2008 2013: 380–89). What no one wants to talk about is what she actually wanted: a cunt, a man, a house, and normal fucking life. Whatever intuition she may not have had about gender as a “managed achievement” was put toward a down payment on a new dishwasher (Garfinkel 1967). If there's anything Agnes “reveals” about gender, it's that actually existing normativity is, strictly speaking, impossible. Norms, as such, do not exist. (If Gender Trouble knew this, it did a poor job explaining it.) That doesn't mean that norms don't structure people's desires; what it means is that the desire for the norm consists, in terms of its lived content, in nonnormative attempts at normativity. Agnes was a nonnormative subject, but that wasn't because she was “against” the norm; on the contrary, her nonnormativity was what wanting to be normal actually looked like. Like most of us, Agnes was making do in the gap between what she wanted and what wanting it got her.

We can argue, and people have, about whether queer theory is possible without antinormativity (Wiegman and Wilson 2015). But whatever comes after trans studies—can I suggest transsexual theory?—will be impossible with antinormativity. The most powerful intervention scholars working in trans studies can make, at this juncture within the academy, is to defend the claim that transness requires that we understand, as we never have before, what it means to be attached to a norm—by desire, by habit, by survival.

Why did we build the normative in the first place? It is not the inherent condition of the cosmos for there to be norms and houses and interiors: we built them from the nonnormative and multivocal substance of the Real. It is the peculiar aberration of the cis to vandalise and depart from norms – that variant of shelter – rather than to reinvent, expand, and seek them out. By this example – that of trans writing’s unconventional motion toward reality versus the more common away-motion – we are shown the difference of the genres, the idiosyncrasy of secular magical realism versus other forms of speculative fiction, and why this genre is, indeed, a realism. I.e., it is a realism because of its motion toward reality, which is that site of sense and authority in which realist fictions are set, or in which they strive to be set.

Now, at the opening of the awaited update, fixed on the swirling redgreyblack gumball of his former homeplanet, Success’s eyes twitched and, twitch by twitch, the gumball marbled and glistened, glossy, green, and blue. Fall. As though never fallen. The vision would not stir. Success screamed at the mockery of it, asked to be mediscanned, but everybody saw it, and scans showed no medical abnormalities in any of the crew. Fall had returned and, rushing upon them hours ahead of schedule, here surged the triple eclipse. Automatically, as it did, their own energy diverted to backup, and so – in the general confusion among the crew of The Transitor – the Destroyers pinged them first, bypassing their netshields and telestepping a boarding party into their command centre. Guns were levelled. Cooperation was urged. But the boarding party stressed they wanted to talk, not fight, and as every crewmember recognised and – so, so deeply – trusted the intruders’ leader, it was easy to comply. The Transitor’s crew forfeited their weapons and, in silence, awaited Suicide Mary’s next words. To their shock, she did not lower her gun, instead aiming it at the stoic, yet reddening face of Success Maximum. “Tell them,” she said.

“It makes no difference,” said Success.

“If it makes no difference…”

“It’s not like I planned it. I trusted my gut and my crew trusted me.”

She sank a bullet into that gut and the old captain winced, but didn’t groan. “What’s your gut telling you now?” When he stared on, unspeaking, she explained, “This is not the Prime Universe. There is no Prime Universe. It is his universe. The Altaverse is one of uncountably many cosmoses, but it was into that universe he stitched himself and, there, stole aboard The Transitor, tampered with the telestepper, and shifted me into a cosmos he thought empty and, because it was empty, inescapable, ensorcelling you so – stitching you so – into his universe. I give a little, not much, but some credence to superstition, and in all but one of the universes I’ve traversed in search of you, my dear crew, Fall endures – as it does in our home-universe. And that’s how I escaped the empty universe he trapped me in – for, you see, it was not empty. One world endured. Fall. Top-Tech! What do you call it when a thing is true for all but one scenario in a billion?”

“Ah,” Top-Tech glanced at Success, then back at Suicide, and therein she recognised her real commander, “An asymptotic fact, ma’am!”

“And what’s another kind of fact?”

“Ma’am, there is none!”

“And, Top-Tech, what’s that tell us about my homeworld?”

“Always and forever Fall endures, ma’am, in this and every timeline!”

“You fucking heard her, everybody. Now, you,” weapon levelled at Success’s face, “you must think you’re going to click that Interthreader attached to your wrist, that secret bit of Marfish tech you’ve been keeping from everybody, and yeah, it would work, and we might never find you, and you’d cause more ruin, pursue the destruction of ever more Falls out of your ugly resentment, but the thing is – because of that – well, there’s no clean way about this, so...” Captain Suicide Mary discharged her firearm, twice, into the impersonator’s frontal lobe, now a heap on the command-centre floor. “I’m taking this ship back,” said Suicide, “and then we’re all going home.”

But Success Maximum’s neural lacing was spread across his entire nervous system, and though the core processor was blown, there remained flickers of his consciousness, enough to will some last few words through the oral interface. “Fall is gone, though. These asymptotes are nothing but math and language. Look out the window.” Everyone looked, and realised the turquoise wonder they beheld truly was Fall – the return to their home-universe already complete. “You see the dust, the black hell that’s there? That’s my reality. It’s real.” Success, the wreckage of him, could not rise or see what they saw. “Truth doesn’t change across universes, and it’s true in mine. It’s a fact. In my universe, I am a fact.”

“Actually,” said Suicide, “truth does change across universes, and now, you’re in mine.”

“The people who destroyed my Fall exist. They’re out there. They… it… massacre… trillions… my people…”

“I’m sorry, Mr Maximum, but that’s just not the case.”

With the least ceremony permissible under galactic law, Success’s remains were pulped, fed to a recycler, and dumped in a donations-bin outside a clone-research lab. That done, Suicide took the command-seat, activated the ship-wide intercom, and announced, “As we return, I’ve got to say, whatever your expectations of Fall or the changes it’s undergone, you better prepare yourself to have those expectations… wildly exceeded.” That was the last clump of dialogue, Sam ending the update with a description of The Transitor sailing slow at first, then blurring as the shields activated, then all at once vanishing into the nebula-ridden sky.

People asked the obvious questions, but, after hearing, again from Sam’s trans woman web designer-friend, about Josephine’s sudden death, for the most part left it alone. The opera tied up quickly, its twenty-year saga ending with little ado. “Josephine,” Sam wrote on his blog some months later, in answer to the outstanding mystery of how his own life inspired that section, “said this was her way of fixing the relationship, something she, in her gut, felt was the only way. In a note. She did not take her own life, I want to be clear, but she wanted to die and part of why she died… Her feeling was part of why she died. Which, I know, doesn’t make sense. But I mean every word.” There were more words, things Sam wanted to express, things he was good at expressing, or supposed to be, ways around the central thesis, to the effect, in any case, Josephine dying put something away, for Sam, some brittle head-shape, hoary where it struck the inmost contours of the self, the mind, the unconscious, bouncing, bouncing, sticking, sinking and sliding off, dripping all through. Instead, the words were “no matter how Josephine died, what matters is it’s fixed. The shape’s gone. It’s not all so bright anymore.” Sam hit send and the thing sent. Spine sunk in a stack of cushions, spread-out stiff on the floor of the lounge, the laptop sighed and stared back. Deliberately and with great care, as if fearful of attention, some forgotten magic drew down the eyelids. So great was this magic’s power over Sam and so delicate its grace that, when oblivion came for her, she did not even notice.
